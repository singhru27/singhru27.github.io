A/B Testing Analysis

The Problem

For any business, creating and ensuring that products are well-received and well-liked by users is always a top priority. Failing to adapt and meet the wants and needs of consumers can lead to backlash (in the case of the recent new Google suite icon rollout) or even rapid loss of users (MySpace).

One of the most effective ways to conduct user research before actually deploying interface changes is A/B testing.

A/B testing involves a randomized experiment, in which users are presented two slightly different versions of the exact same interface. Metrics such as time to completion (how long the user takes to complete a task) and return rate (how often the user returns to a landing page) are measured for each version.

After collecting the data, statistical tests are conducted to determine whether the difference leads to statistically significant results.

For this project, I investigated a series of changes to a basic shopping cart website. This kind of website is used by hundreds of thousands of businesses, and user retention is key for the success for many small businesses (especially as the world transitions to a primarily online shopping society). The Hypotheses and Research

Time To Completion

  • Null Hypothesis: The two websites will not have difference in time to completion
  • Alternative Hypothesis: Website A will have a lower Time to Completion than Website B
  • Justification:
    • Website A has all the cacti laid out in columnar fashion, and the “Cart” button is located right below the last cacti. This allows the user to add cacti to their cart, and then select the checkout option with very little mouse movement (since the Cart icon is right below the last button.
    • Website A follows the natural scan pattern of the reader and places the button right at the end of the scan pattern. Website B, on the other hand, has a Cart button located nearly half a page away from the last cacti. This forces the reader to break from their natural scan pattern and re-look at nearly all the cacti before locating the button (which also might entice them to select more cacti, further adding to their time to completion)

Return Rates

  • Null Hypothesis: The two websites will not have difference in return rates
  • Alternative Hypothesis: There will be a difference in return rates between the two websites
  • Justification:
    • Website A contains the pictures, number of each cactus in cart, and price of each cactus in a rough column. This allows for fairly easy comparisons of the different products (since they are all vertically aligned), and also makes adding together the numbers a bit easier (since our current addition methods taught to most students since grade-school tend to teach addition in this columnar fashion)
    • This may improve for easier and more decisive comparisons between the items available, as well as more instinctive calculations of total prices (which makes a difference, since customers are instructed to add at least $150 of items)
    • Comparatively, Website B is laid out in horizontal fashion and prices aren't as easily compared (since they come at the end of text strings that require reading). This may lead the user to need to go back and forth from the checkout page to the current page, because of a comparative lack of ease of comparison leads the user to use the aggregation displayed in the checkout page to guide their decisions

Data Collection

Data was primarily collected from undergraduate college students enrolled in a User Interfaces and User Design class. These students were instructed to add $150 worth of their items to their respective carts, then to check out

To ensure representation of a diverse demographic range in the AB tests (as this website will potentially be used by individuals beyond just college students), this website and task was administered to several members of my family (ranging from age 17 to 80)

Through these efforts, 25 individual website interactions were recorded and logged. 11 individual used Website A, while 14 individuals used Website B

Conclusion/Parting Thoughts

Perhaps one of the biggest takeaways from this project was that conducting tests with high power and low chance of statistical error requires a large volume of data. With this project we were fortunate to have such extreme results that we were able to reject the null with only 25 samples, but it required a nearly 66% difference in means between the two groups - a number that may be difficult to reach.

Scan patterns are crucially important towards improving the user experience (and specifically user efficiency). Action buttons placed at the end of user scan patterns resulted in a difference of nearly 7 seconds for time to completion.

The results of this test may be limited in its generalizability because of the skewed sample on which it was conducted. Online cacti buyers are most likely not 20 year old college students - having a sample representative of the true population is crucial for obtaining proper results.

AB Testing tends to perform at its best when it is conducted on unassuming users (i.e natural browsers of a website who don't even know that a test is being conducted). When individuals are told that they are being tested, it appears that they become hyperaware of their actions and may change their behavior.
